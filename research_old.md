My research sits at the intersection of natural language processing, corpus construction, and linguistically informed modeling. Since the mid-2000s, a central thread has been the relation between expressive annotation schemes and practical, data-driven systems.

## Main directions

### Robust and multilingual parsing

A large part of this work focuses on syntactic parsing for languages and domains that are difficult to model with standard supervised approaches: morphologically rich languages, freer word-order settings, out-of-domain text, and highly variable user-generated content.

### Treebanks and annotation

Many of the projects in the archive revolve around the design, creation, extension, and exploitation of annotated corpora. The objective is not only to benchmark systems, but also to make it possible to train more realistic models for difficult genres and languages.

### Beyond surface syntax

Another recurrent theme is moving from shallow or surface-oriented syntax toward richer predicate-argument structure and graph-based representations, especially when syntax alone is not enough to capture the linguistic phenomena of interest.

### Language models and safety-oriented evaluation

The most recent section of the archived site highlights a shift toward language models, including French-centered modeling efforts, low-resource settings, generated-content detection, sociolinguistic awareness, and questions related to robustness, bias, and misuse.

## Research environment

My research were carried out first in the Alpage team-project and then in Almanach.
